What is Total Internal Reflection?

When a ray of light passes from an optical medium of greater refractive index ($n_1$, such as water or glass) to an optical medium of lower refractive index ($n_2$, such as air), it refracts away from the normal.

As the angle of incidence inside the denser medium increases, the angle of refraction in the second medium increases too. At some point the refracted ray "runs along" the interface (90° refraction). Beyond this, there is no refracted ray at all — all the light is reflected internally. This is Total Internal Reflection (TIR).

The Critical Angle (θc)

Definition: The critical angle is the angle of incidence in the medium of greater refractive index (1st medium) that corresponds to an angle of refraction in the medium of lower refractive index (2nd medium) equal to 90°.

Deriving the Formula

Apply Snell's Law at the critical condition, where $\theta_1 = \theta_c$ and $\theta_2 = 90°$:

$$ n_1 \times \sin\theta_1 = n_2 \times \sin\theta_2 $$

At critical angle: $\theta_1 = \theta_c$, $\theta_2 = 90°$, so $\sin 90° = 1$

$$ n_1 \times \sin\theta_c = n_2 \times 1 $$
$$ \theta_c = \sin^{-1}\!\left(\frac{n_2}{n_1}\right) $$

$\theta_c$ (Critical Angle)

$\sin^{-1}(n_2/n_1)$

$n_1$ — 1st medium

Denser (larger $n$)

$n_2$ — 2nd medium

Less dense (smaller $n$)

Worked Example

A ray passes from glass ($n_1 = 1.5$) into air ($n_2 = 1.0$). Find the critical angle.

Step 1 — Apply the formula:

$\theta_c = \sin^{-1}(n_2 / n_1) = \sin^{-1}(1.0 / 1.5)$

$\theta_c = \sin^{-1}(0.667) \approx 41.8°$

✅ For glass-air interface, θc ≈ 41.8°. Any ray inside the glass hitting the surface at more than 41.8° will undergo total internal reflection.

الزاوية الحرجة بالعربي
الزاوية الحرجة ($\theta_c$) هي أصغر زاوية سقوط في الوسط الأكثر كثافة ضوئية بتخلي الانعكاس الكلي يحصل.

القاعدة:
• لو زاوية السقوط أقل من $\theta_c$: جزء بيعدي وجزء بيتعكس.
• لو زاوية السقوط = $\theta_c$: الضوء بيتكسر بزاوية 90° ويمشي على الواجهة.
• لو زاوية السقوط أكبر من $\theta_c$: انعكاس كلي — مفيش ضوء بيعدي!

Application: Optical Fibers

Structure

a) The optical fiber is a fine tube of transparent material (glass or plastic) with a high refractive index (small critical angle).
b) When a ray of light enters the tube, it suffers successive total internal reflections (since the angle of incidence is greater than the critical angle), until it emerges from the other end without loss of energy.

Animated: Total internal reflections inside an optical fiber

Important Note

Each fiber is coated with a thin layer of material of lower refractive index:
(a) To keep the light rays inside the core without escaping.
(b) To reflect any rays escaping from the core — keeping the intensity of light intact without decrease.

Functions (Applications)

🏥

Medical Endoscopes

Fiber-optic devices are used in endoscopes which can be inserted into the bronchial tubes, bladder, colon, and other organs for direct visual examination. A bundle of fibers can even be enclosed in a hypodermic needle for studying tissues and blood vessels beneath the skin.

📡

Communication Systems

Fiber optics are used to transmit a modulated laser beam. The rate of information transmission is proportional to the frequency — and visible/infrared light has much higher frequency than radio waves, so a single fiber can carry enormous amounts of information.

📞

Telephone, TV & Internet

Fiber-optic cables play an important role in long-distance telephone, television, and internet communication. They can be made thinner than copper wire, so more fibers can be packed into a cable — carrying more distinct signals. Because they are electrical insulators, they are immune to lightning and electrical interference.

⚡

Why More Info Per Fiber?

Each crest of the wave can represent a binary digit (1 or 0). The number of digits transmitted per second is proportional to the frequency. Infrared and visible-light waves have much higher frequency than radio waves — so a modulated laser beam can transmit an enormous amount of data through a single cable.

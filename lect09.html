ELECTRIC & MAGNETIC FIELDS

Electromagnetic Waves

Electromagnetic Waves are vibrating electric and magnetic fields in phase, perpendicular to each other, and perpendicular to the direction of their propagation.

By the end of the 19th century, several types of electromagnetic waves were discovered:

1) Radio Waves
Discovered by Heinrich Hertz while investigating electrical sparks.
2) Infrared & Ultraviolet
These lie just beyond the red and violet ends of the visible spectrum.
3) X-rays
Discovered by Wilhelm Röntgen, produced when electrons collide with a metal target.
4) γ-rays (Gamma)
Discovered by Henri Becquerel while investigating radioactive substances.

Properties of Electromagnetic Waves

  • They are vibrating electric and magnetic fields that move through space or a medium.

  • They are transverse waves only.

  • They do not need a material medium to travel through (they can travel in a vacuum).

  • They propagate in a vacuum (free space) at the same speed (c) = $3 \times 10^8$ m/s, but vary in frequency and wavelength. They travel slower in material media.

Light & The Speed of Light

Light is an Electromagnetic wave that occupies a small range in the electromagnetic spectrum.
• Range of wavelength of visible light is between 400 nm and 700 nm.
• Visible light consists of 7 colors (Red, Orange, Yellow, Green, Blue, Indigo, Violet).

James Clerk Maxwell showed that the speed $c$ of electromagnetic radiation in a vacuum was independent of the frequency. All types travel at the same speed in a vacuum: $3 \times 10^8$ m/s.

$$ c = f \lambda $$
Crucial Physics Rule

When light travels from a vacuum into a material medium (like glass or water), its speed decreases, but its frequency remains the same. Since $c = f\lambda$, if the speed decreases while frequency is constant, the wavelength must decrease.

Light Rays vs Wavefronts

To describe light direction, we use rays. A ray is an imaginary line along the direction of travel of the wave. Rays are always straight lines normal (perpendicular) to the wavefronts.

THE SPECTRUM

The Electromagnetic Spectrum

All electromagnetic waves travel at light speed, but vary immensely in frequency, production, and use.

Explore the Spectrum

Visible Light

Wavelength: 700 nm to 400 nm

It is the most familiar form of electromagnetic waves, detected by the human eye.

Production Electrons in atoms emit light when moving to lower energy levels.
Detection The eye, Photocells, Photographic film.

INTRODUCTION

Concept of Motion

The object is said to be at rest when the position of the object does not change with respect to the time. While the object that changes its position as the time passes, the object is said to be in motion.

Motion: is the change in the position of the object with respect to a fixed point as the time passes.

Motion can be classified into two types:

1. Translational (Translatory) Motion

The motion which is characterized by having a starting point and end point in a certain path. Such as motion in a straight line, projectile motion, and transportations.

2. Periodic Motion

Motion of an object that regularly returns to a given position after a fixed time interval, such as vibrational (oscillatory) motion, wave motion and circular motion.

📝 Note

Circular motion: motion that repeats itself over equal intervals of time — e.g., Earth around its axis and around the Sun.

Vibrational motion: motion that repeats itself over equal intervals of time — e.g., a simple pendulum going forth and back.

WAVE MOTION

What is a Wave?

Many of us enjoy watching waves on the surface of water pushing a fishing float or a boat up and down, or even making waves by throwing a pebble in a pond or still water. Each pebble becomes a source of disturbance in the water, spreading waves as concentric circles.

You encounter waves all the time in your daily life. For example, you can see because of visible light waves and hear because of sound waves.

Wave: is a disturbance that propagates and transports energy in the direction of its propagation through medium or space.
⚡ Key Principle

Wave motion: energy is transferred over a distance, but matter is not.

The water molecules don't travel with the wave — they oscillate up and down while energy moves forward.

Examples of Waves:

  • Waves on the surface of liquid, sound waves, waves on stretched strings
  • Electromagnetic radiations, such as light, x-rays, radio waves, infrared and ultraviolet radiation

TWO MAIN TYPES

Mechanical vs. Electromagnetic Waves

The world is full of waves, the two main types being mechanical waves and electromagnetic waves.

🌊

Mechanical Waves

It is the disturbance that propagates through a medium, such as sound waves, water waves.

(Some physical medium is being disturbed; in our pebble example, elements of water are disturbed.)

Examples: water waves, sound waves

📡

Electromagnetic Waves

It is the disturbance that propagates through a medium or space, such as light waves, television signals, x-rays, radio waves and γ-rays.

(They do not require a medium to propagate)

Examples: light, radio waves, X-rays, TV signals

CHARACTERISTICS

Wave Characteristics

The following important definitions about waves and wave motion:

Sinusoidal wave showing displacement, amplitude, crests, troughs, and wavelength

x

1) The Displacement x

The distance of a point on the wave from its undisturbed position or equilibrium position.

A

2) The Amplitude A

The maximum displacement of any point on the wave from its undisturbed position.

📝 Note

The amplitude of a wave on the sea is measured in units of distance. The greater the amplitude of the wave, the louder the sound or the rougher the sea.

λ

3) The Wavelength λ (lambda)

The distance from any point on a wave to the next exactly similar point (the two points are in phase) (e.g. crest to crest).

The Greek letter lambda. The wavelength of a wave on the sea is measured in units of distance.

$$ \lambda = \frac{\text{total distance}}{\text{number of waves}} $$
T

4) The Period (periodic time) T

(The time taken for one complete oscillation of a point in a wave)

OR

(It is the time taken for a point to move from one particular position and return to that same position, moving in the same direction)

It is measured in units of time.

$$ T = \frac{\text{total time}}{\text{number of waves}} = \frac{1}{f} $$
f

5) The Frequency f

(The number of oscillations per unit time of a point in a wave)

The frequency of a wave is measured in units of reciprocal time (Hz — Hertz).

$$ f = \frac{\text{number of waves}}{\text{total time}} = \frac{1}{T} $$

FORMULAS

Key Wave Formulas

Wavelength

$$\lambda = \frac{d}{n}$$

d = distance, n = number of waves

Frequency

$$f = \frac{n}{t} = \frac{1}{T}$$

n = number of waves, t = time

Period

$$T = \frac{t}{n} = \frac{1}{f}$$

Inverse of frequency

COMPARISON

Mechanical vs. EM Waves

Mechanical Waves Electromagnetic Waves
Need a material medium for propagation.Do NOT need any material medium for propagation.
Produced due to the vibrations of particles of the medium.Caused due to change in electric and magnetic fields.
Particles vibrate along or perpendicular to direction of propagation.Particles vibrate perpendicular to direction of propagation.
Have low speed.Have high speed (light speed = 3×10⁸ m/s).
Formed from longitudinal and transverse waves.Formed from transverse waves only.
Examples: water waves, sound waves.Examples: radio waves, light, X-rays.

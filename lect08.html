The Nature of Sound

Sound is a form of energy that travels in the form of a longitudinal mechanical wave (compressions and rarefactions).

Important: Sound can travel through any medium (solid, liquid, gas), but it cannot travel through a vacuum. There is no sound in outer space!

Frequency Range

  • • Audible Sound: Humans can hear frequencies between 20 Hz and 20,000 Hz (20 kHz).
  • • Ultrasound: Frequencies above 20,000 Hz.
  • • Infrasound: Frequencies below 20 Hz.

Pressure Variations

  • Sound is produced by the vibration of sounding bodies.
  • • Compression: Region of increased pressure where particles are close.
  • • Rarefaction: Region of decreased pressure where particles are apart.
Notes on the Speed of Sound (in gases)

a) Density: Speed of sound is inversely proportional to the square root of the medium's density ($\rho$):

$$ v \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{\rho}} $$

b) Temperature: Higher temperature $\rightarrow$ lower density $\rightarrow$ sound travels faster.

Intensity of Sound

The intensity of a wave is defined as the rate of energy transmitted (i.e., power) per unit area at right angles to the wave velocity.

$$ \text{Intensity (I)} = \frac{\text{Power (P)}}{\text{Cross Sectional Area (A)}} $$

Intensity is measured in watts per square meter ($W \cdot m^{-2}$).

Intensity and Amplitude

The intensity of a wave $I$ is directly proportional to the square of its amplitude $A$:

I $\propto$ A²

Meaning: If one wave has twice the amplitude of another, it has four times (2²) the intensity! It carries energy at four times the rate.

Why does sound fade away?

The intensity of a wave generally decreases as it travels because of two main reasons:

  • 1

    Spreading of the wave: As spherical waves spread out from a point source, the area increases, so the energy per unit area (intensity) and amplitude decrease.

  • 2

    Absorption or Scattering: The medium itself absorbs some of the wave's energy and turns it into heat.

Measuring Frequency

We can measure the frequency of sound waves using a Cathode-Ray Oscilloscope (c.r.o.).

How it works:

  1. A microphone captures sound waves.
  2. The microphone converts them into a varying electrical voltage.
  3. The voltage is displayed on the c.r.o. screen as a trace.
  4. The screen behaves like a voltmeter charting voltage over time (x-axis).

By knowing the horizontal scale (time-base control), we can find the time of one complete wave (Period $T$), and then calculate Frequency $f = 1/T$.

INTERFERENCE

Standing (Stationary) Waves

Consider a string stretched between two clamps. If you send a continuous specific sinusoidal wave to the right, it will reflect off the end and travel back to the left.

The interference of two identical sinusoidal waves moving in opposite directions produces standing waves.

All standing wave patterns consist of nodes (zero amplitude) and antinodes (maximum amplitude).

Frequency of a Stretched String

A stretched string fixed at both ends can form standing wave patterns depending on the frequency. It works in whole segments ($n = 1, 2, 3...$). The length $L$ fits an exact number of half-wavelengths ($L = n\frac{\lambda}{2}$).

$$ f = \frac{n}{2L} \sqrt{\frac{F_T}{\mu}} $$
  • n: The number of segments (1, 2, 3...)
  • L: Length of the string (m)
  • FT: Tension force (Newton)
  • μ (mu): Mass per unit length (kg/m)
Harmonics:
  • • $n = 1$: Fundamental mode or First harmonic (one segment).
  • • $n = 2$: Second harmonic (two segments).
  • • $n = 3$: Third harmonic (three segments).
